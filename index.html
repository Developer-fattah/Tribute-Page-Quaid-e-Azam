Muhammad Ali Jinnah

1876-1948

(“Father of the Nation”)

Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah (/ˈɑːli ˈdʒɪnɑː/; born Mahomedali Jinnahbhai; 25 December 1876 – 11 September 1948) was a barrister, politician, and the founder of Pakistan. He served as the leader of the All-India Muslim League from 1913 until Pakistan's creation on 14 August 1947, and then as Pakistan's first Governor-General until his death. He is revered in Pakistan as Quaid-e-Azam ("Great Leader") and Baba-e-Qaum ("Father of the Nation"). Born in Karachi (then part of British India), Jinnah studied law in London and became a prominent lawyer in Bombay. He initially advocated for Hindu-Muslim unity and was a member of the Indian National Congress, but later became the leading voice for the Two-Nation Theory, which argued for a separate Muslim state in the subcontinent. His leadership during the Pakistan Movement culminated in the creation of Pakistan, an independent nation for Muslims. Jinnah's legacy as a statesman, constitutionalist, and advocate for democracy and religious freedom remains central to Pakistan's identity.
